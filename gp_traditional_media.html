H1 General Paper · Paper 1 · Traditional Media

Assess the view that traditional media platforms are no longer relevant.

Stand: Extent Question
BPs: 6 body paragraphs
Structure: 2 Supporting → Transition → 4 Opposing + Rebuttal
"The physical format is dying; the institutional model is not — and it is the model that makes traditional media relevant."

Full Essay

Introduction The rapid rise of digital platforms has radically transformed how information is produced, distributed and consumed. In comparison to the immediacy and algorithm-driven reach of social media, traditional media platforms — such as print newspapers, terrestrial television and radio — appear increasingly outdated. Traditional media refers not merely to physical formats but to an institutional model characterised by professional editorial gatekeeping, legal accountability, structured newsroom hierarchies and long-standing reputational capital. To claim that they are "no longer relevant," however, is to argue that they have lost meaningful impact, influence and value in shaping public discourse. While traditional media has undeniably lost its former dominance and monopoly over information flows, it remains relevant in distinct but evolving ways — particularly in credibility, agenda-setting authority and institutional legitimacy. Its influence may be diminished and reconfigured, but it is far from obsolete.
BP1 — Structural Decline (Supporting) Certainly, traditional media has suffered a significant erosion of audience share and economic power. Younger generations increasingly consume news through short-form digital platforms where speed, virality and personalisation trump editorial gatekeeping. Advertising revenue has migrated toward algorithm-driven platforms that offer targeted marketing, weakening the financial foundations of legacy outlets. In this sense, traditional media is less relevant than it once was in commanding attention or setting the pace of information dissemination. However, declining dominance should not be conflated with irrelevance. Relevance must be measured not merely by popularity, but by depth of influence and structural role within society.
BP2 — Long-Term Structural Threat (Supporting) Critics may argue that this influence is temporary and unsustainable. As artificial intelligence, citizen journalism and decentralised media models expand, the gatekeeping function of traditional institutions could diminish further. If younger audiences continue disengaging from print and broadcast formats, traditional platforms may struggle to maintain financial viability and cultural relevance. This counterargument is significant because it addresses long-term structural decline rather than short-term adaptation.
BP3 — Credibility as Structural Relevance (Opposing + Rebuttal) A key reason traditional media remains relevant lies in its credibility and accountability. Unlike many digital platforms, established media institutions operate under professional journalistic standards, legal liability and editorial oversight. This institutional framework produces a level of trust that algorithm-driven platforms often struggle to replicate. During moments of crisis — whether public health emergencies, elections or geopolitical conflicts — audiences frequently turn to established broadcasters and newspapers for verified updates rather than unfiltered social media feeds. The relevance here is not rooted in speed but in reliability. In a fragmented information ecosystem plagued by misinformation, credibility itself becomes a scarce and valuable resource, reinforcing the continued importance of traditional media institutions.
BP4 — Agenda-Setting & Knowledge Production (Opposing + Rebuttal) Moreover, traditional media retains agenda-setting power, even within a digital ecosystem. While social media amplifies content, much of the investigative reporting and in-depth analysis that fuels online discourse originates from legacy newsrooms. Digital platforms often circulate, comment on or react to stories first uncovered by traditional journalists. This suggests that traditional media still performs a foundational function in knowledge production. The architecture of public debate may have shifted online, but its intellectual scaffolding frequently rests upon the reporting infrastructure of conventional outlets. Thus, rather than being replaced, traditional media has become structurally embedded within the digital information chain.
BP5 — Adaptation & Institutional Persistence (Opposing + Rebuttal) Yet this prediction underestimates the adaptive capacity of institutions. Traditional media has increasingly embraced hybrid models, integrating digital subscriptions, podcasts, livestreaming and social media engagement into their operations. In doing so, they retain their core strengths — investigative rigour, editorial standards and institutional legitimacy — while participating in digital distribution networks. Relevance, therefore, may no longer reside in the physical format of newspapers or broadcast schedules, but in the enduring authority of the institutions behind them. The medium evolves; the institutional influence persists.
BP6 — Contextual Variation (Opposing + Rebuttal of Absolute Claim) Furthermore, relevance must be assessed across demographic and geopolitical contexts. In regions with limited internet infrastructure or among older populations, radio and television remain primary information sources. Even in highly digitised societies, official announcements delivered through televised addresses often carry symbolic authority and national weight that fragmented digital posts cannot replicate. Such broadcasts function not merely as information delivery mechanisms but as ritualised moments of collective attention, reinforcing their continued socio-political significance.
Conclusion Ultimately, traditional media platforms are no longer unrivalled, but neither are they irrelevant. Their dominance has waned, and their modes of engagement have transformed. However, through credibility, agenda-setting capacity, institutional legitimacy and adaptive integration into digital ecosystems, they continue to shape public discourse in substantive ways. The view that traditional media is "no longer relevant" confuses declining monopoly with extinction. In reality, relevance has shifted from control over speed to authority over substance. As long as societies value verified information and structured accountability, traditional media — albeit in evolving forms — will remain integral to the media landscape.
